What is SatNOGS?

SatNOGS is a global network of satellite ground stations. But let's start with the basics.

Earth has many satellites

6000+ of them!

Satellites can be categorized in many ways, like orbit and size. One really interesting category for us are cubesats.
Cubesats are small satelites, size 10x10x10cm that can be easily constructed using of the self hardware.
Because its easy to construct them, we have seen a huge rate of launches during the past 4 years. What's interesting about cubesats is that most of them are using amateur radio bands for their transmissions. Radio bands: non encrypted, free to air, scientific payloads, status beacons.

Low Earth Orbit Satellites

We don't see them all the time!

Unfortunately, most of them are low earth orbit satellites (200-2000km). That means that their orbital period is quite short (couple of hours). That means that for those that want to listen to them, they only have a short overpassing time window (for a specific location).

Ground Stations

big and scary

In order to listen to satellites we use ground stations. A ground station is basically antennas mounted to a rotator that can track the position of the satellite in the sky and receive the signal for a specific frequency. As you can see, ground stations like this can be super expensive and under heavy regulations, so, In order to tackle this problem, the first thing that we needed to solve this was to provide an open source cheap reference design. But that could only solve the cost issue, but we wanted also to address the global coverage issue.

Let's create a Network

easy, right?

Design: users have access to our global network and submit satellite observation jobs. Then our network calculates the observation and sends the jobs to our global deployment so our ground stations could track and communicate to satellites.

No unified way to do this

Let's do everything from scratch :)

... but in a modular way

When we came up with the initial architecture we found out that there was no unified way to do this. So we decided to create everything from scratch. This is not an easy task so we focused on implementing everything in a modular way. Specifically we wanted to reuse existing components to tackle the initial problem. Let's dive into our stack.

Rotator

3D Printed, cheap, reliable

Rotator is a mechanical device that enables antennas to move on azimuth and altitude dimensions.
Here you can see our first deployment. This is our v2 rotator in the rooftop of our hackerspace in Athens and a UHF hellical antennan mounted on it.
For the receiving we are using everyones favourite DVB-T. The off the shelve solution caused some instability in our setup (PPM errors) so we modded the oscillator for extra stability.

Permanent vs. Portable

We have both!

A ground station can be permaent or portable and we have both designs.
Here you can see our tripod design for portable setups.
We also provide a design for permanent setups using a radome. This is an optional one mostly usefull for extreme weather conditions.

The Client

modular, extensible

After the network schedules the observation, the client is polling the network and gets the jobs assigned to it. Now client waits until the time comes to start tracking. Calcules satellite's position and moves the rotator accordingly.

SatNOGS DB

Crowdsourced, community

An interesting issue that we encountered was that there was no source to get satellite's transceiver data. Transceiver data = Frequency, Modulation, Baud rate etc. That's why we created satnogs db, a crowdsourced satellite transceiver database based on community's contributions.

Thriving community

So overally our stack looks like this. You can use different combinations of antennas (yagi, helical, uhf, vhf) You can either mount it to our reference design or nay commercial rotator. On the client side you can use the embedded platform that you want (beaglebone green). Then you could either run SatNOGS client to orchestrate the satellite tracking, other commercial software or their open source implementations. Finally you can decide if you want to send the data back to the network or just use the ground station offline.
As you can understand, this is a really complicated problem that we are trying to tackle and wouldn't be possible to achieve anything without the community help.
